> Strategy Description

It has been observed that, from a market trend perspective, 80% of the time is spent in a range-bound state. Grid trading strategies are designed to address such ranges. While there are multiple ways to implement grid trading, fundamentally it involves setting up a relatively stable position-taking strategy where trades are executed whenever price fluctuations meet certain conditions. For instance, if prices drop by 5%, we could add 20% of the total capital; after five such additions, our capital would be fully utilized. Similarly, if prices increase by 5%, we could reduce our initial holdings by 20%. After five increases or decreases, this completes a cycle. This is the basic idea behind grid trading.

Today, QuBan Quantitative introduces a quantified strategy that builds upon the concept of grid trading but includes some enhancements to potentially achieve an annualized return of up to 130%. QuBan refers to it as the Convenience Store Strategy because it imagines the trader operating a small convenience store. The trader aims for a fair price in the market; if prices exceed this fair value, they sell their goods; conversely, if prices are below this fair value, they buy more. In addition, there is a small notebook to record previous transaction prices. If current prices fall by 5% from the last transaction price, another purchase can be made, and vice versa. To prevent infinite operations, trading will cease when cash levels drop below or rise above 10%.

Here's how it works in detail:
Step One: Observe the volatility of the commodity to find a fair value indicator, such as a certain moving average (30-minute line with a 20-period), or the middle band of Bollinger Bands. By default, set an initial position at 50% and record the transaction price.
Step Two: If prices drop by 3%, issue a buy signal; if they rise by 3%, issue a sell signal. Record the transaction price. If current prices fall by 5% from the last transaction price, again issue a buy signal; if they rise by 5%, issue a sell signal and record the transaction price.
Step Three: Based on current position size, decide how to act when receiving a buy order. Do not act if position is between 10% and 90%; but record the transaction price. Each operation only buys 20% or 10% of the capital, avoiding infinite operations.

This strategy is called the Single Product Convenience Store Strategy because it deals with only one commodity. As an improvement direction, we hope to introduce multiple commodities for rotation, even back-to-back short selling for hedging.
Let's run a backtest first. We choose ETH as our commodity, with a trading period from January 1, 2019, to October 10, 2019—a period marked by significant increases and decreases in price.

The backtesting results are quite good, achieving an annualized return of 130%, along with generating 1651 yuan in transaction fees. This is a strategy that traders and exchanges should be pleased with.
However, the maximum drawdown is still relatively high at around 30%. The main drawdowns occur during periods when the commodity experiences significant declines. It makes sense to consider this as the strategy is anchored to trading commodities; if prices fall significantly, it might mean holding positions at a higher price point that haven't yet been sold off to the market, but over time, this should be recouped.

                wantOper=0;
